System Weaknesses & Interpretive Maneuvers

Every systematic theology is a human attempt to harmonize an infinitely complex, divine revelation. Because we are finite, every theological system has "rough edges"—specific biblical texts that seem to naturally contradict the system’s core premise. When a system encounters its "problem verse," it must employ hermeneutical maneuvers to make the verse fit. Critics call this eisegesis (reading ideas into the text); defenders call it the analogy of faith (interpreting unclear verses in light of clear ones). Here is an honest look at the vulnerabilities of each system.

1. Pelagianism

The Core Weakness: It blatantly ignores the visceral, biblical reality of human bondage to sin and makes Christ merely a moral example rather than a necessary Savior.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • Ephesians 2:1: "And you were dead in the trespasses and sins."
  • Romans 5:12: "Sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin..."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: Pelagians force-fit these texts by stripping them of their ontological weight. They argue that "dead" in Ephesians 2 simply means "socially alienated from God" or "caught in bad habits," not lacking the capacity for life. For Romans 5, they argue for imitation rather than imputation—Adam didn't pass down a sinful nature; he just set a very bad example that everyone happens to copy.

2. Semi-Pelagianism (Erasmus)

The Core Weakness: It compromises the absolute sovereignty of God in salvation. By insisting that man must take the first tiny step, God becomes a responder rather than the sovereign initiator. Grace becomes necessary, but not sufficient on its own.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • John 15:5: "Apart from me you can do nothing."
  • Romans 9:16: "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: Erasmus looked at John 15:5 and argued that "nothing" is hyperbolic. He force-fit it to mean: "Apart from me you can do nothing perfectly or meritoriously enough to earn final salvation." For Romans 9, Semi-Pelagians often argue that Paul is talking about God's historical plan for nations (Israel and Edom), not the eternal salvation of individual souls.

3. Arminianism

The Core Weakness: The entire system hinges on "universal prevenient grace" (a grace given to every human to restore their free will back to neutral). The weakness is that this specific concept is arguably nowhere explicitly taught in Scripture; it is a theological deduction made to protect human responsibility.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • Romans 8:29-30 (The Golden Chain): "For those whom he foreknew he also predestined... called... justified... glorified." (Notice no one drops out of this chain).
  • John 6:37: "All that the Father gives me will come to me."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: Arminians use a maneuver called Prescient Election. They argue that "foreknew" in Romans 8 means God looked down the corridors of time, foresaw who would use their free will to choose Him, and then predestined them based on that foreseen choice. Reformed theologians argue this forces the text to say "God foreknew their actions," when the Greek text actually says God foreknew the persons ("those whom he foreknew").

4. Reformed / Augustinian (Calvin & Luther)

The Core Weakness: The system can make God appear to be the author of sin, or a tyrant who creates people for the express purpose of damning them. It also struggles to explain how God can genuinely offer the Gospel to all people if He has only died for the elect.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • 1 Timothy 2:4: "God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth."
  • Matthew 23:37: "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem... How often would I have gathered your children together... and you were not willing."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: To handle 1 Timothy 2:4, Reformed theologians typically use one of two maneuvers. We argue that "all people" means "all types of people" (kings, peasants, Jews, Gentiles), not every individual on earth. Alternatively, we use the "Two Wills of God" distinction: God has a revealed will (He morally desires all to repent) but a secret decretive will (He has eternally decreed to save only the elect).

5. Neo-Orthodoxy (Barth)

The Core Weakness: By making Jesus Christ the only truly predestined and reprobate man, it blurs the line between the believer and the unbeliever, leaning heavily into Universalism. It also treats historical biblical events (like the Fall) as "theological sagas" rather than literal history.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • Matthew 25:46: "And these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life."
  • John 3:36: "...whoever does not obey the Son shall not see life, but the wrath of God remains on him."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: Barth refused to explicitly endorse universalism, calling hell the "impossible possibility." However, he force-fits verses about eternal damnation by arguing they are terrible threats of what would happen if Christ had not taken all reprobation upon Himself. The warnings are treated as existential crises meant to awaken us, rather than literal descriptions of a populated hell.

6. Theology of Hope (Moltmann)

The Core Weakness: It so heavily collectivizes and politicizes the Gospel that individual, spiritual salvation can be completely swallowed up by social liberation. Traditional categories of heaven, hell, and personal piety become secondary to political eschatology.

The "Problem" Scriptures:

  • John 18:36: "Jesus answered, 'My kingdom is not of this world.'"
  • Philippians 3:20: "But our citizenship is in heaven."

The "Force Fit" Maneuver: To prevent the Gospel from being an inward, escapist religion, Moltmann must maneuver around verses that seem to point believers away from the earth. The force-fit is to argue that "not of this world" refers to the origin of the Kingdom, not its destination. He argues that focusing on a spiritual "citizenship in heaven" is a misreading that leads to apathy; instead, heaven is meant to aggressively invade and humanize the present political earth.
